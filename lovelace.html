Ada Lovelace - writer, mathematician, computer programmer

Augusta Ada King, Countess of Lovelace, was a 19th century mathematician and is often regarded as the first computer programmer. She worked on Charles Babbage’s Analytical Engine, and it was he who called her “The Enchantress of Number”. Lovelace’s most important contribution to computer was her realization that a computer could be used in applications beyond calculation.

Timeline of her Life

  • 1815 – Born in London, England.

  • 1828 – At age 12 becomes fascinated with flight and wants to fly. Studies bird anatomy and tries to construct wings.

  • 1829 – Paralyzed due to complications from measles.

  • 1831 – Gains the ability to walk using crutches.

  • 1832 – Becomes interested in mathematics and develops her mathematical abilities.

  • 1833 – Has an affair with a tutor and attempts to elope with him but is caught and returned to her mother.Becomes close friend with her tutor Mary Somerville who introduces her to Charles Babbage.

  • 1835 – marries William, 8th Baron King, becoming Lady King.

  • 1838 – King is made Earl of Lovelace, which makes Ada the Countess of Lovelace

  • 1842 – translates Luigi Menabrea’s article on Babbage’s Analytical Engine. Her appended notes explain a method for calculating a sequence of Bernoulli numbers. This method leads to her being considered the first computer programmer.

  • 1852 – Died (age 36) of cancer in London, England.

“[The Analytical Engine] might act upon other things besides number, were objects found whose mutual fundamental relations could be expressed by those of the abstract science of operations, and which should be also susceptible of aptations to the action of the operating notation and mechanism of the engine...Supposing, for instance, that the fundamental relations of pitched sounds in the science of harmony and of musical composition were susceptible of such expression and adaptations, the engine might compose elaborate and scientific pieces of music of any degree of complexity or extent.” -Ada Lovelace
